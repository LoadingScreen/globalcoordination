Goals and Structure

The goals of this document are:

  1. Spark a discussion that refines this document and expands it with further insights and ideas;

  2. Establish a framework and ideas pool for fixing global game-theoretic problems that builds on existing work in relevant fields (game theory, economics, political science, cryptography, etc) and tries to help with 21st century problems (such as x-risk from emerging technology);

This document has the following structure:

  1. First, in the Past Successes and Past Failures chapters we look at various examples of how humanity has dealt with game-theoretical challenges, such as the tragedy of the commons, in the past;

  2. Following that, the General Strategy and Strategy Notes chapters propose factoring the Nash equilibrium design challenge into two sub-problems: preference discovery and preference promotion;

  3. The Game Structure chapter categorises the components (e.g., agents, resources, technology, etc.) of the “games” whose equilibria we might want to nudge;

  4. Then the New Agents, New Theory, New Technology, New Memes chapters flesh out those categories with components that are potentially important yet sufficiently new (or even non-existent but plausibly available in the near future) that they haven't seen much use in the context of global mechanism design;

  5. The complementary Novel Risks chapter lists some possible ways the application of the new technologies and other components might make things worse;

  6. The Example Interventions chapter gives a few speculative examples of global game-theoretic interventions that the new technologies might enable;

  7. Finally, the Conclusion and Next Steps chapter summarises the document and proposes a list of next actions.
