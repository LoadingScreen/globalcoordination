General Strategy

One way to frame the problem we're tackling here is that, save in some isolated cases{%fn%}, humanity is not very strategic in pursuing its own long term interests. After all, as of 2017, we spend about 3 orders of magnitude more on advertising tobacco {%fn%} than we spend on x-risk research{%fn%} {%fn%}.

This problem, in turn, factors into 2 challenges that are better tackled in isolation:

  1. Figuring out what humanity's long term interests are (“preference discovery”), and
  2. Steering the world towards outcomes that correspond to those preferences (“preference promotion”).

(You can think of these challenges as rough generalisations of legislative and executive processes in democracies.) The most popular mechanisms for (large scale) preference discovery in use today are democracy and free markets. Many believe that they seem to work better than the alternatives. However, they are ultimately limited:

Democracy (Interestingly, democracy is a coordination mechanism that was itself enabled by technological advances: modern democracy was enabled by the printing press [wiki-print] and secret ballot [wiki-ballot] — h/t Allan Dafoe, and Athenian democracy used sophisticated physical technology as well [dibbell-athens].) relies on non-corrupt officials and a sufficiently competent electorate, and it is clear that in some countries (e.g., Iraq and Afghanistan) naive democratization can be catastrophic. There is a correlation between a country's level of democracy and levels of wealth, education, and health, suggesting that these might be prerequisites to democracy {% fn %} {% fn %}, but making strong inferences from this observation is complicated by the problems inherent in reasoning about any macro-level correlation. And while they work well enough in many domains, democracies have a hard time addressing exotic or highly complex topics such as x-risk reduction {% fn %}. Even if the electorate can understand the relevant analysis, focus on more emotionally salient issues can dominate policy discussion. Also, and relatedly, democracies appear increasingly vulnerable to malicious “election hacking” {% fn %}.

While democracy is intended as a mechanism by which the preferences of the citizens control the actions of the state, this is not so simple in practice. For one, the rulers of democracies have every incentive to influence the preferences of the voters. In order to acquire and maintain power, they can and frequently do employ means such as propaganda, state-sponsored media, and state-regulated education to alter the citizens' preferences to match their own.

Another complication with democracy is that modern states are representative democracies which measure people's preferences about who they want in office, not their preferences about policy. This overweights preferences that are conductive to being associated with charismatic candidates.

More fundamentally, as psychological research indicates {% fn %}, people are often poor at predicting what they want from the future (Anecdotal example: by popular demand, Estonia abolished daylight saving time in 2000, only to reverse that decision 2 years later as people discovered, after all, that they valued the long summer evenings (plus the convenience of being in sync with the rest of Europe) above waking up at constant biological time), not to mention what policies would best serve them. In the absence of enlightened self-interest, democratic processes often decay to affiliation signalling {% fn %} {% fn %}.

Democracies are interesting in that they not only work to discover preferences, they also work to promote them, and these two capabilities are not separated — an attempt to sway and shape preferences is also an attempt to enact them (by getting votes and promoting popular legislation).

Free markets, while working wonderfully across many domains as discovery and promotion mechanisms for valuable products and services (they can also deal with the “faster horse” phenomenon {% fn %} better than democracy can), have their own limitations. For example, markets often fail to value common pool resources (such as non-polluted environment or x-risk reduction) {% fn %} and don't necessarily track human values in contexts such as military technology {% fn %}. In general, they value human well-being only indirectly{% fn %}.

Even worse, markets can have catastrophic failure modes (e.g., “bubbles” {% fn %}) where short term interests are promoted at the expense of long term sustainability [Indeed, in my opinion, the biggest disservice of capitalism might be the creation of a false sense of security: markets promote the development of powerful technologies that improve the lives of people in the short term, while also introducing global catastrophic risks from accidental or deliberate misuse of such technologies — “error or terror” scenarios, as Martin Rees{% fn %} calls them].

The existing mechanisms for global preference promotion are even worse: the UN is often accused of being very ineffective (at least in the face of the growing difficulty of the challenges that it faces{% fn %}) {% fn %}, so with a few exceptions (e.g., the Montreal Protocol{% fn %}), the world struggles to enforce policies even backed by near consensus{% fn %} {% fn %} {% fn %}. While there are proposals to increase the power of existing global governance structures{% fn %}, this will be difficult because, above the state level, the world is basically an anarchy between competing “global tribes” {% fn %}. Sadly, as Joshua Greene{%fn%} writes in Moral Tribes{% fn %}, “the very same moral thinking that enables cooperation within groups undermines cooperation between groups, \[because morality\] evolved as a device for successful intergroup competition. \[...\] We need a metamorality”.


Hopefully we could do much better in the future by explicitly considering a) the game-theoretic aspects of global processes and b) the new global technologies and movements that did not exist when democracy and free markets were invented. There may be ways to engineer {% fn %} stable Nash equilibria that reward actors who help with global preference discovery and promotion — not unlike free markets today reward people and businesses to research and develop beneficial consumer products and services.

In particular:

  1. For preference discovery, use technology and philanthropic actors to incentivise, automate, corruption-proof and scale existing empirically working mechanisms, such as polls {% fn %}, focus groups, sentiment analysis {% fn %}, courts, etc., and perhaps explore novel mechanisms {% fn %};
  2. For preference promotion, use technology to incentivise people and institutions to align their goals and actions with the output of the preference discovery mechanisms — for example, by giving people the tools to easily expose deviations (“defection”) from such tracked preferences. More generally, we need coordination tools and organisations that can prevent (or at least detect and fix) bad Nash equilibria (e.g., various arms races) in various domains.
